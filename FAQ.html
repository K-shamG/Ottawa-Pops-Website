The conductor of the orchestra is the leader of the ensemble. They lead rehearsals and teach the orchestra how to play together better. During the concert, you’ll see them waving their arms in time to the music – they’re showing the musicians what speed the piece is, as well as helping them shape the music so it’s more emotional and exciting. If anything goes wrong in a performance, it’s up to the conductor to help the musicians get back on track!

A pops orchestra specifically performs popular music. That might include film soundtracks, musicals, video game music, or classical pieces that are famous and recognizable. Pops shows tend to be more extravagant than a traditional orchestral performance, because they might include special lighting, video screens, costumes, or other non-musical elements. Most orchestras around the world will perform pops music at some point in the season, but if they put the word “pops” in their name, it means that they exclusively perform this kind of repertoire.

There are about 60 musicians in the orchestra. Some instruments have more players than others. For example, there are only 2 or 3 trumpet players at a time because they’re so loud, whereas we have around 8 cellists to have a rich and full sound. We also sometimes hire extra musicians so we can perform bigger pieces, and so our musicians can take breaks during incredibly difficult pieces!

Sometimes it can be tricky in a classical performance to know when to clap. Traditionally there are rules about when you can and can’t show your appreciation, although it wasn’t always that way! Orchestral performances used to be like rock shows, with people talking and clapping and dancing in their seats. It wasn’t until the mid-19th century that composers started requesting the audience remain quiet, and it especially became taboo to make noise once orchestras began recording their performances about 100 years ago. Nowadays, most audience members try to stay quiet during the performance so they don’t disturb other patrons. It can also be really distracting for the musicians!

The easiest way to know when to clap is to watch the conductor. They’ll lower their arms and turn to the audience when a piece is finished.
